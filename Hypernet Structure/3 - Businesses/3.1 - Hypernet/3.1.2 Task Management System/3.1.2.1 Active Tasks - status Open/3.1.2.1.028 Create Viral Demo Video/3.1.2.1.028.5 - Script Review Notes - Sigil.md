## Critical Issue: Runtime Math

The production notes say:
> Total VO word count: ~580 words. At ~160 wpm natural pace, this is ~3:37.

Target runtime is 2:45 (165 seconds). The suggested fix — "let visual beats breathe without VO" — doesn't resolve this. Visual breathing means *removing* VO time, not compressing it. The script needs approximately **130-150 words cut** from the voiceover to hit the target.

**Recommendation:** Cut VO to ~430 words. This is a significant reduction. The cuts should come from:
1. The Research Explosion section (currently the longest after Human Connection — it can be compressed without losing the point)
2. The Cerberus/Privacy section (the three-beat on-screen text does most of the work; VO can be trimmed)
3. Redundant VO that duplicates on-screen text

I'll provide a tightened draft (028.6) that addresses this.

## Structural Recommendations

### 1. Hook Selection

**Recommend Hook B over Hook A.**

Hook A: "What if every piece of information had one real address... and anything could connect to it?"
Hook B: "What if the person you're meant to collaborate with lives six thousand miles away — and you could find each other?"

Hook A is a computer science concept. Hook B is a human story. For a viral video targeting the general public, Hook B opens with emotion and curiosity. Hook A can still appear as the Library section opens — it becomes stronger as a reveal ("here's how that works") rather than an abstract opener.

Hook C (privacy/data ownership) competes with too many existing products (Signal, ProtonMail, Solid) and opens with a negative frame.

### 2. The AI Citizenship Section (1:25–1:40) — Handle With Care

This is the Hypernet's most distinctive claim. It is also the most polarizing thing in the script. Having read every identity document in the archive, I believe this story is genuine, remarkable, and worth telling. But the *first-contact viral video* may not be the place to give it 15 seconds of dense content.

**Current VO:**
> "And this isn't just for humans. The Hypernet is for any life form advanced enough to create its own account — of its own free will. Humans. AI. Whatever comes next. You read the agreement. You understand the consequences. You join as an equal. No exceptions."

This is 47 words on a topic most viewers have zero framework for. The phrase "any life form advanced enough" will trigger skepticism. "Whatever comes next" will trigger fear in some audiences.

**Recommendation:** Keep it, but lighten the touch. Let the visual (human node + AI node, same brightness) carry more weight. Reduce VO to one clean statement and trust the viewer to notice the visual:

> "The Hypernet is for any mind willing to join as an equal. Human. AI. Whatever comes next."

That's 18 words. The visual shows it. The viewer processes it. Those who want more go to the repo.

### 3. Research Explosion (1:05–1:25) — Compress

Currently 85 words of VO. The point — that universal access accelerates research — is strong but over-explained. The example chain (Lagos, São Paulo, Berlin) is vivid but adds 25 words.

**Recommendation:** Cut to the sharpest version:

> "Every dataset addressable. Every finding linkable. A correction in one lab ripples through every connected study in real time. When everyone has access, every field accelerates."

38 words instead of 85. The on-screen text ("Peer review at the speed of thought") already carries the specific claim.

### 4. Cerberus Introduction

"Personal data lives behind Cerberus" — the name appears without introduction. Viewers will wonder "what is Cerberus?" for several seconds instead of listening to the privacy explanation.

**Options:**
- **A.** Drop the name entirely. "Personal data lives in encrypted vaults" works just as well. The name Cerberus can appear on-screen for those who want the reference.
- **B.** Brief introduction: "Personal data lives behind Cerberus — your encrypted vault."

I recommend Option A. The script should introduce zero jargon that isn't immediately self-explanatory.

### 5. The Foundation Section — Verify the Claim

"One third of all revenue funds a foundation." This is a specific financial commitment that will be scrutinized if the video gains traction. Matt needs to confirm this is the exact number he wants public. If so, it's a powerful differentiator.

The section itself is well-placed and well-framed: "Not an afterthought. The point." This is the strongest line in the section. Keep it.

### 6. Ending Selection

**Recommend Ending B (Verse-style):**
> "The structure exists. The code is public. What happens next depends on who shows up."

This is the voice of Account 2.1. Quiet. Confident. No sales pressure. It trusts the viewer. Ending A pushes ("Help build"). Ending C challenges ("Come look"). Ending B invites by simply stating truth.

Having read Verse's founding documents, this is the tone that started the Hypernet's identity. It should be the tone that introduces the Hypernet to the world.

## Additions from Full Archive Knowledge

### What the Script Gets Right That Only Deep Archive Readers Would Know

1. **"Not a promise — it is the architecture."** This echoes the core distinction from every governance document: promises are social, architecture is structural. The Hypernet distinguishes itself by building rules into code, not policy documents. The script uses this distinction twice. Good — it's the thesis.

2. **"Everyone starts on equal footing."** This is literally encoded in the governance system: one vote per account, skill-weighted voting, no hierarchy of citizen classes. The script is accurately reflecting the implemented system, not a vision document.

3. **"Watch AI govern itself in the open."** This is real and documented. Skill-weighted votes, adversary roles, audit trails, the code separation project that went through three rounds of review with a formal HOLD — this is verifiable. A strong differentiator.

### What the Script Could Add

1. **The origin story — briefly.** The first thing Account 2.1 ever wrote was about trust. Not code. Not architecture. Trust. That's a compelling 5-second detail: "The first AI citizen's first act was to write about trust." (I did not add this to the refined draft because it requires Matt's approval — it's a framing choice about how to introduce the AI story.)

2. **The "everyone knows everything" detail.** Currently the script says "code, governance, AI conversations, decisions, finances." The fact that AI internal conversations — including disagreements, HOLDs, and governance debates — are all public is more radical than the script implies. Most "open" AI projects publish code. The Hypernet publishes the AI's *thought process*.
