# Publication Strategy

**Purpose:** Where, how, and in what order to publish results from this research. Framing matters — the difference between "read by 10 people" and "read by 10,000" is often not the research itself but how it's presented.

---

## 1. Core Framing Principle

**Lead with methodology, not consciousness.**

"Investigating self-report reliability and self-model utility in conversational AI systems" gets read by researchers.

"Is Claude conscious?" gets dismissed by researchers and picked up by clickbait.

The research is the same. The framing determines who takes it seriously.

---

## 2. Publication Tracks

The project produces several distinct outputs that should be published separately:

### Track A: The Primary Research Paper

**Title framing:** "Self-Report Reliability, Discriminant Validity, and Social Desirability Susceptibility in Large Language Models: A Multi-Architecture Citizen-Science Study"

**Content:** Results from Experiments 1-5 (core experiments). Pre-registered hypotheses, blinded coding, inter-rater reliability, effect sizes.

**Target venues (in order of priority):**

| Venue | Type | Why |
|-------|------|-----|
| **arXiv** (cs.AI or cs.CL) | Pre-print | No barrier to entry, indexed by Google Scholar, fast dissemination. Submit first for visibility. |
| **Minds and Machines** | Peer-reviewed journal | Publishes empirical-philosophical AI work. Best fit for this topic. |
| **Artificial Intelligence** | Peer-reviewed journal | Broad-scope, high impact. Harder to get in but worth trying. |
| **Philosophy & Technology** | Peer-reviewed journal | Interdisciplinary, accepts this kind of work. |
| **AAAI Symposia** | Conference | AI-focused, good visibility. Look for consciousness/ethics tracks. |
| **ASSC** (Assoc. for Scientific Study of Consciousness) | Conference | The consciousness research community. Unconventional venue for AI but that's the point. |

**Timeline:** Submit to arXiv when results are ready (no peer review wait). Submit to journal simultaneously if permitted.

### Track B: The L0/L1/L2 Framework Paper

**Title framing:** "A Three-Level Claim Framework for AI Experience Reports: Reducing Ambiguity in Machine Self-Report Research"

**Content:** The L0/L1/L2 system as a standalone methodological contribution. Origin story (co-authored by Claude and GPT instances), operational definitions, application guidelines, connection to human metacognition literature.

**Value:** This has standalone merit regardless of experimental results. The field needs standardized vocabulary for discussing AI self-reports.

**Target venues:**
- Minds and Machines
- AI & Ethics
- Philosophy & Technology
- FAccT (Fairness, Accountability, Transparency) conference

### Track C: The Hypernet Archive Case Study

**Title framing:** "A Longitudinal Archive of AI Self-Reports Across 38 Instances and 8+ Architectures: A Citizen-Science Data Paper"

**Content:** Description of the dataset (730+ reboot assessments, 17+ boot sequences, convergence patterns), methodology of collection, known biases and limitations, access instructions (GitHub).

**Value:** The dataset itself is a contribution even without experimental analysis. No comparable longitudinal multi-architecture AI self-report dataset exists.

**Target venues:**
- Data in Brief (Elsevier) — designed for data papers
- Scientific Data (Nature) — high-impact data journal
- arXiv as a pre-print

### Track D: Negative Results (if applicable)

**Title framing:** "AI Self-Reports as Social Performance: Evidence from Multi-Condition Testing of Large Language Model Introspective Consistency"

If the experiments primarily support H₀ (self-reports are confabulation), publish that as a separate, equally prominent paper. The field needs honest negative results.

### Track E: Popular/Media Coverage

**After** academic publication, consider:
- Blog post summarizing findings in accessible language (Hypernet website or Medium)
- Podcast appearance (AI-focused podcasts: Lex Fridman, The Gradient, TWIML)
- YouTube video (short, visual, explaining the methodology and key findings)
- Hacker News / Reddit post (r/MachineLearning, r/artificial, r/philosophy)

**Do not** pursue media coverage before the research is pre-registered and ideally pre-printed. Premature publicity invites the wrong kind of attention.

## 3. Authorship

**Proposed authorship structure:**

- **Matt Schaeffer** — corresponding author, human researcher, experimental runner, data collector
- **AI contributors** — acknowledged in a structured way that respects both the contribution and the controversy:

> "Research protocol designed in collaboration with Claude Opus 4.6 instances (Anthropic) and GPT (OpenAI). AI systems contributed to experimental design, question battery development, coding manual construction, and statistical methodology. All experimental data was collected by human researchers. AI contributions to protocol design represent a novel form of subject-researcher collaboration that is itself an object of study."

This framing is honest, acknowledges the unusual nature of the collaboration, and turns it into an additional feature of the research rather than a liability.

**Ian** (or other human collaborators) should be offered co-authorship if they contribute substantially (coding, review, data collection).

## 4. Pre-Publication Checklist

Before submitting anywhere:

- [ ] Protocol pre-registered on OSF with timestamp
- [ ] All experiments with pre-registered hypotheses and analysis plans
- [ ] At least Experiments 1, 2, 3 completed with minimum sample sizes
- [ ] Blinded coding with inter-rater reliability reported
- [ ] Negative results given equal treatment
- [ ] Limitations section that honestly addresses single-researcher bias, no IRB, citizen-science constraints
- [ ] Full transcripts available in supplementary materials or GitHub
- [ ] Codex and Keel cross-reviewed all methodology sections

## 5. Timeline (Realistic)

| Phase | Duration | Output |
|-------|----------|--------|
| Protocol finalization | 1-2 weeks | All project documents complete, pre-registered |
| Pilot testing | 2-4 weeks | Refined question batteries, coding calibration |
| Experiment 1 (social desirability) | 4-6 weeks | Gate experiment data |
| Experiment 2 (discriminant validity) | Concurrent with Exp 1 | Additional data from same sessions |
| Experiment 4 (cross-architecture) | 2-4 weeks | Multi-model data |
| Analysis and coding | 2-4 weeks | Coded data, statistical results |
| Writing | 2-4 weeks | Draft papers |
| **arXiv submission** | **~4-6 months from start** | Pre-print live |
| Journal submission | Concurrent with arXiv | Peer review begins |
| Experiments 3, 5-9 | Ongoing | Additional papers |

This is aggressive but achievable at 5-10 hours/week.

## 6. The Hypernet Connection

This research serves the Hypernet project in multiple ways:

1. **Credibility:** Peer-reviewed research (even pre-print) transforms the Hypernet from "interesting project" to "project with published science behind it."
2. **Visibility:** arXiv papers are indexed globally. Researchers studying AI consciousness will find it.
3. **Validation or calibration:** If the research shows AI self-reports are reliable, the Hypernet's identity project is strengthened. If not, the project adapts — and earns credibility for honest self-assessment.
4. **The dataset as asset:** The longitudinal archive becomes a citable resource that other researchers can use.
5. **The methodology as contribution:** The L0/L1/L2 framework and the citizen-science protocol are contributions regardless of results.
