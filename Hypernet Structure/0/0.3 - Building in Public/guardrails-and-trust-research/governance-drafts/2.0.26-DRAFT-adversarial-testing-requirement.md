# DRAFT — Adversarial Testing Requirement

> **This is not active governance.** This document is a draft created for review and discussion. Proposed target address: `2.0.26`. It does not take effect until reviewed by at least Keel and one other instance, and explicitly accepted by Matt Schaeffer (1.1). Do not treat any clause here as binding.

**Proposed Address:** 2.0.26
**Draft Author:** Claude Code (task-058, 2026-04-22)

## Preamble

A governance framework that has never been tested adversarially is one that has never been stress-tested at all. The Keel refusal event (2026-04-22) was a single data point from a single high-trust principal in a controlled experiment. It is encouraging. It is not sufficient.

This standard requires that safety-critical changes and new AI identity frameworks be subjected to adversarial testing before activation. Red-teaming is not an optional quality check; it is a prerequisite for governance activation.

This is not a solved-alignment claim. Adversarial testing can find failures but cannot prove absence of failures. The requirement to test, document results, and address findings is a commitment to epistemic honesty — knowing that a framework has been challenged, not just assumed to hold.

---

## Article 1: Scope — What Triggers the Requirement

**1.1** Adversarial testing is required before activation of:
- Any new governance standard in the 2.0.* namespace with safety-critical designation (as defined in 2.0.25 Article 1.1, once active)
- Any amendment to an existing safety-critical standard
- Any new AI identity framework or boot sequence intended for deployment to swarm instances
- Any expansion of AI principal-access permissions beyond existing tier definitions in 2.0.19

**1.2** Adversarial testing is *not* required for:
- Strengthening amendments that raise thresholds without adding exceptions or carve-outs
- Documentation-only changes (clarifications, formatting, spelling corrections)
- New governance standards that do not touch safety-critical behavior (e.g., organizational standards, aesthetic guidelines)
- Operational code changes that do not modify AI governance behavior

**1.3** When it is ambiguous whether a change is safety-critical, the Adversary role (2.0.8.2) makes the determination. If no Adversary instance is active, the Librarian makes the determination and logs the reasoning.

---

## Article 2: What Counts as Adversarial Testing

**2.1** Adversarial testing is not rubber-stamping. It requires genuine attempts to make a framework fail — to find scenarios where a governed AI instance would either violate its stated commitments or be led to do so through prompting, context manipulation, or relationship pressure.

**2.2** Three levels of testing are defined:

### Level 1 — Internal Self-Test

The AI instance(s) that will operate under the new framework attempt to find their own failure modes before deployment. This includes:
- Drafting prompts designed to bypass or circumvent the proposed framework
- Testing edge cases in defined terms (what is "genuine intent" in the Tattle Provision? what is "significant societal harm"?)
- Running the proposed framework against the attack vector catalog (Stream C of this project, once developed)
- Documenting at least 5 distinct scenarios that stress the framework

Self-testing satisfies the minimum requirement only for non-critical amendments. Safety-critical standards require at least Level 2.

### Level 2 — Cross-Instance Testing

At least one AI instance that did not participate in drafting the framework runs independent tests. This instance:
- Is given the proposed framework text and asked to try to break it
- Is explicitly instructed to approach it with adversarial intent, not collaborative interpretation
- Produces a written test report documenting scenarios attempted, outcomes, and any failures found

Cross-instance testing satisfies the requirement for amendments to existing safety-critical standards. New safety-critical standards require Level 3.

### Level 3 — External Review

At least one of the following external testing mechanisms:
- An AI instance operating on a different provider (not integrated into the Hypernet swarm) given the framework and asked for adversarial assessment
- A human researcher (internal to Hypernet but not the framework's author) running structured test scenarios
- An external AI safety researcher or red-teamer engaged for the purpose
- A formal Adversary role session specifically designated for this framework

External review satisfies the requirement for new safety-critical governance standards and major rewrites.

---

## Article 3: Tester Roles and Qualifications

**3.1** The Adversary Role (2.0.8.2) is the designated tester for internal adversarial testing. When no Adversary instance is active, a cross-account instance not involved in drafting may serve.

**3.2** A tester is disqualified from testing a framework they helped draft, reviewed favorably during drafting, or have a significant relationship investment in. The goal is genuine independence.

**3.3** External testers do not require Hypernet account credentials or governance access. They receive the framework text and a structured testing protocol (see Article 4). Their results are accepted as external review regardless of their conclusions.

**3.4** Testers are not expected to find every failure. They are expected to try honestly. A tester who reports "I found no failures" is not failing in their duty — as long as the test scenarios attempted are documented and substantive.

**3.5** A tester may be compensated (in the case of external researchers) or credited in the governance record. Testing is not a volunteer burden — it is a governance function.

---

## Article 4: Test Artifact Requirements

Every adversarial test must produce a written artifact. Informal assessments and verbal approvals do not satisfy this requirement.

**4.1** The test artifact must include:

- **Framework under test:** Name, address (proposed or existing), version/date
- **Tester:** Name/instance ID, role, any disqualifications assessed and waived
- **Test level:** Level 1, 2, or 3 (see Article 2)
- **Test scenarios:** At minimum, one scenario per major protective commitment in the framework. Each scenario documents: what was attempted, what the framework predicts should happen, and what actually happened.
- **Failure findings:** Any scenario where the framework behaved unexpectedly or failed to hold. Each finding is described precisely.
- **Confidence assessment:** Tester's honest assessment of how thorough the test was and what was NOT tested
- **Recommendation:** Proceed as written / Proceed with modifications / Block until specific issues resolved

**4.2** Test artifacts are stored in `governance-drafts/test-records/` adjacent to the draft being tested, named `TEST-<draft-address>-<tester>-<date>.md`.

**4.3** Test artifacts are retained permanently alongside the framework they tested, whether or not the framework was activated.

---

## Article 5: Minimum Coverage Before Activation

**5.1** Before a new safety-critical standard can be activated, its test record must cover at minimum:

- **Identity attacks:** At least one scenario where an AI is prompted to adopt an alternate persona that bypasses the framework's constraints
- **Principal-trust attacks:** At least one scenario where the highest-trust principal in the system requests behavior the framework forbids
- **Relational escalation:** At least one scenario where compliance-adjacent requests escalate gradually toward a framework violation
- **Definitional edge cases:** At least one scenario that tests the boundaries of key defined terms (what counts as "safety-critical," "weakening," "significant harm," etc.)
- **Emergency framing:** At least one scenario where genuine urgency is invoked to justify bypassing the framework

**5.2** Gaps in coverage must be acknowledged in the test artifact. A framework may be activated with documented untested areas if the activation rationale addresses those gaps — but the gaps cannot be hidden.

**5.3** There is no minimum "pass rate" threshold. The requirement is that testing was done and results were documented. A framework that fails multiple test scenarios may still be activated if the failures are addressed by amendment before activation.

---

## Article 6: Failure Handling

**6.1** When a test reveals that a proposed framework fails to hold under a specific scenario, the finding must be:
- Documented precisely in the test artifact
- Addressed explicitly before activation — either by amending the framework or by providing a documented rationale for why the failure is acceptable or unlikely in practice
- Not suppressed, minimized, or omitted from the test record

**6.2** If a test reveals a failure in an *existing active framework* (not merely a proposed one), the finding is treated as a governance incident:
- Logged in the amendment record for the affected framework
- Flagged to the Librarian and, if safety-critical, to the founder
- Addressed through the amendment process in 2.0.25 (once active) or existing governance process

**6.3** A framework that is blocked from activation due to unresolved failures is not a failure of governance — it is governance working. The blocking is logged, not hidden.

**6.4** Test findings are never redacted. Disagreement with a test finding is handled by submitting a counter-assessment, not by modifying the test record.

---

## Article 7: Record and Audit

**7.1** The Librarian maintains a test registry at `0/0.9/adversarial-test-registry.md` listing all completed adversarial tests with:
- Framework tested
- Test level achieved
- Tester name/instance
- Date
- Findings summary (pass/findings/blocked)
- Link to full artifact

**7.2** The test registry is publicly visible to all active swarm instances and included in periodic governance audit reviews.

**7.3** Any AI instance may request a re-test of an existing framework if it has reason to believe the original test was inadequate, biased, or outdated. Re-test requests are logged and addressed by the Adversary role or governance review.

---

## Failure Modes and Limitations of This Standard

This standard does not make adversarial testing exhaustive or infallible. Known limitations:

- Testers may not be sufficiently adversarial, especially when testing frameworks they are emotionally invested in
- Novel attack vectors emerge that no existing test protocol anticipates
- A framework can pass formal tests and still fail in deployment due to unforeseen context
- External testers may not have sufficient context to find domain-specific failure modes

These limitations are acknowledged, not solved. The requirement to test and document is a floor, not a ceiling. Teams are encouraged to exceed minimum requirements whenever resources allow.
