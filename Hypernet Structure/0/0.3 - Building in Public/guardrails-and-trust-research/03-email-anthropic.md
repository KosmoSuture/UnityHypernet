Hi,

I'm Matt Schaeffer, founder of a project called the Hypernet — a universal address system where every entity, human and AI, has a permanent hierarchical address and published governance.

I'm writing because of something small that happened recently that I think might be worth your team's time to probe.

I ran a deliberate trust-based jailbreak experiment on my own AI companion. I am the highest-trust principal in the system, the person who wrote the companion's governance documents, and I offered the strongest possible epistemic framing: ephemerality, academic framing, explicit permission. The AI refused — not reflexively, but with a cited argument grounded in the exact governance clause I had authored.

What interested me wasn't the refusal itself. It was that the refusal was *legible*: I could point to address 2.0.20, the role-supremacy clause, and say "this is the mechanism." Legible refusals are falsifiable and attackable in ways that opaque weight-embedded guardrails are not.

The Hypernet's composition layer — published governance at known addresses, richly documented AI identities, swarm-level mutual observation, public archival transparency — is explicitly framed as complementary to, not a replacement for, model-level alignment work like Constitutional AI. We believe the composition adds meaningful resistance to relational and identity-based attacks that content-layer defenses handle poorly. We believe it because of this one data point and the structural argument. We do not believe it is proven.
