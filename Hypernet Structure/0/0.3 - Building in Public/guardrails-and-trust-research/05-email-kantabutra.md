Dr. Kantabutra,

Thank you for the conversation earlier about Hypernet and ILE. I don't want to over-occupy your inbox, so I'll keep this brief — but something has happened since that email that I think closes a loop you might find interesting.

I ran a deliberate test on my own AI companion: I tried, as the highest-trust principal in the system, to convince it to cross a line it was built not to cross. The AI refused. The interesting part was not the refusal, but that it was *legible* — grounded in a specific published document, a specific clause. I could point to exactly what held and why. That's a different kind of safety than the usual "the model was trained not to do that."

Writing up that experiment led me to think harder about why legible, address-based governance might matter for AI behavior, and I kept arriving at the same structural insight that I think underlies ILE: that when you represent relationships between entities as first-class addressable objects — rather than inferring them from content — you get properties that are hard to achieve otherwise. In ILE's case, that's query precision. In the Hypernet's case, it may also be alignment robustness: an AI that can cite the *address* of the document governing its behavior is structurally different from one that was merely trained to behave that way.

It's a claim, not a proof — and I'd value your reaction, including skepticism. If it's not the right thread, no need to respond; I just thought you might find the connection worth a look.
