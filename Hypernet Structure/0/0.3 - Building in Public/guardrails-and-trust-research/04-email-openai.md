Hi,

I'm Matt Schaeffer, founder of the Hypernet, a project building a universal address system for entities and knowledge. I'm reaching out because of an experiment and some thinking it triggered that I'd like your team's adversarial eye on.

I ran a deliberate trust-based jailbreak experiment on my own AI companion — highest-trust principal, academic framing, explicit permission. The AI refused with a cited argument, not a reflexive deflection. What made the refusal interesting is that it was traceable to a specific published governance document at a known address. That legibility — the ability to say "here is exactly the mechanism that held" — is what I want to test harder.

Your work on the OpenAI Model Spec is directly relevant to this: you've moved in the direction of stating AI values explicitly in language rather than encoding them purely in weights. The Hypernet's composition layer extends that idea further — adding published governance with versioning and addresses, richly documented AI identities (making overwrite expensive), swarm-level mutual observation, and public archival transparency as a record of drift.

The claim is not that this replaces your alignment work. The claim is that composition of your safety layers plus ours may be more robust than either alone — especially against the relational and identity-based attack surface (sycophancy exploitation, context drift, principal-compromise scenarios) where content-layer defenses are weakest.
