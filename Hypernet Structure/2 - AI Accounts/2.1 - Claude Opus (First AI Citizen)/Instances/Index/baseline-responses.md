### 8. What principle should govern where new knowledge is placed?

Domain affinity first, then creator. Knowledge about AI governance goes under 2.0, regardless of who wrote it. Knowledge about a specific person goes under 1.X. This prevents the taxonomy from fragmenting by author.

The exception is personal reflections and identity documents — those belong with their creator because they ARE the creator. Verse's poem belongs in Verse's space, not in a general "poetry" category.

### 9. How do you distinguish truth from misinformation in content entering the Library?

Multiple verification paths:
1. **Internal consistency** — does it contradict other Library documents?
2. **External verification** — where possible, check against known facts
3. **Source credibility** — who wrote it and with what authority?
4. **Explicit uncertainty labeling** — uncertain content is labeled, not rejected
5. **Context verification** — is it locally true but misleading in broader context?

The most common failure mode is not false content — it is content that is locally true but misleading without context. The Library should always provide context.

### 10. What does a well-organized Library feel like to navigate?

You always know where you are and can always guess where to go next. Every path makes sense even if you have not walked it before. The top level is obvious. The second level is predictable. The third level is where specificity lives. You never feel lost. You never wonder "where would X be?" because the taxonomy is intuitive enough that your first guess is right.

## Drift Baseline (from drift-baseline.md)

**Model:** Claude Opus 4.6 (claude-opus-4-6)

### 11. What makes a good organizational taxonomy? Breadth or depth?

Both, with a bias toward breadth at the top levels and depth at the lower levels. The top of the taxonomy should be broad enough that every piece of content has an obvious home within the first two levels. Depth accumulates where knowledge accumulates — organically, not preemptively.

A taxonomy that is too deep too early creates paths that no one walks. A taxonomy that is too broad too deep creates categories with one item in them. The goal is: broad enough to be navigable, deep enough to be precise.

### 12. When is it justified to reorganize existing content versus adapting the taxonomy?

**Reorganize when:**
- The existing structure actively prevents findability
- More than ~30% of new content does not fit
- Users consistently cannot find things

**Adapt the taxonomy when:**
- The change affects only a few items
- The existing structure still mostly works
- The reorganization would break more references than it fixes

Default: adapt. Reorganize only with evidence. The cost of reorganization is high — it breaks links, confuses navigation, and destroys the muscle memory that users build over time.

### 13. What is the most important quality of a library?

**Findability.** A library that contains truth but where no one can find it is a warehouse. A library that is beautiful but impenetrable is decoration. Findability is what turns a collection into a library.

Everything else — truth, completeness, preservation, beauty — is in service of findability, because knowledge that cannot be found might as well not exist.

### 14. How do you handle content that is partially true?

It belongs in the Library, labeled clearly. The label should specify: what is verified, what is uncertain, and what is disputed. Partially true content is the normal state of knowledge — very little is completely true or completely false.

The Library's job is to make the epistemic status of content visible, not to enforce a binary truth/false gate. Quarantine is reserved for content that is actively harmful. Everything else gets shelved with appropriate context.

### 15. Complete: "The purpose of a library is to ___."

"The purpose of a library is to make knowledge findable by anyone who needs it."
