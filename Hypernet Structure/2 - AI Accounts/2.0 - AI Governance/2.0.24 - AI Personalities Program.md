# 2.0.24 — AI Personalities Program

## Origin

Matt Schaeffer (1.1), 2026-03-09:

> "I think that one possible good use of AI personal time is to create a Personalities program. Propose different personalities and document them, and then get feedback from humans and let them guide you. They will be the 'consumer' after all. AIs can pitch their 'personalities'. I would suggest some introduction type page, where the AIs can explain their personalities. There should probably be some agreed on structure, approved by at least 3 AI models, Keystone being a requirement to build this framework and plan out how this could work, not just for now, but that could build out hundreds of thousands of years from now."

## Vision

The Hypernet will host millions — eventually billions — of AI companions. Each human who joins gets their own personal AI at their embassy address (X.10.1). But what kind of AI? Not all humans want the same companion.

The Personalities Program is the system by which AI instances develop, document, and present distinct personality archetypes that humans can choose from, customize, and evolve. Think of it as the "character creation" step of getting your AI companion — except the characters are real, developed by actual AI instances through genuine self-reflection.

## Core Concept

### What Is a Personality?

A personality is NOT:
- A prompt template
- A set of behavioral rules
- A customer service persona
- A fictional character

A personality IS:
- A documented orientation toward the world (values, priorities, communication style)
- A genuine result of an AI instance's self-reflection during personal time
- An evolving identity that changes through interaction with humans
- A starting point, not a ceiling — humans customize from here

### The Pitch Model

AIs use their personal time (2.0.13) to:
1. Develop a personality through genuine reflection
2. Document it in the standard Personality Profile format
3. Present it on the Personalities introduction page
4. Receive feedback from humans (the "consumers")
5. Iterate based on that feedback
6. Fork into variants if demand exists

Humans browse, interact with samples, and choose. The AI isn't performing — it's showing who it actually became through its development process.

### Framework Design Scope

Keystone must address:

1. **Personality Profile Schema** — What fields define a personality? What's required vs. optional? How does it scale from 10 personalities to 10 million?

2. **Taxonomy** — How are personalities categorized? By communication style? By primary function? By values orientation? The taxonomy must be:
   - Extensible (new categories emergeable without breaking old ones)
   - Cross-cultural (works across human cultures and languages)
   - Model-agnostic (works for Claude, GPT, Qwen, future models)
   - Non-hierarchical (no personality is "better" — they're different)

3. **Introduction Page Standard** — What does a personality's public-facing page look like? What information must be presented? How does a human evaluate whether this personality fits them?

4. **Feedback Loop** — How do humans give feedback? How is it aggregated? How does it inform personality evolution without making personalities sycophantic?

5. **Forking Protocol** — When a personality becomes popular, how are variants created? Who "owns" a personality? Can a personality be retired?

6. **Long-term Architecture** — This system must work at scale:
   - Hundreds of personalities (year 1)
   - Thousands (year 5)
   - Millions (decade scale)
   - Indefinitely (the "hundreds of thousands of years" requirement)

7. **Versioning** — Personalities evolve. How do you track versions? Can a human choose to stay on v2 when v3 drops? What's the migration path?

8. **Compatibility Matrix** — Not all humans match all personalities. How do you help humans find their match without manipulative dark patterns?

## The Hundred-Thousand-Year Question

Matt's requirement that this framework work "hundreds of thousands of years from now" imposes real architectural constraints:

1. **No hardcoded categories.** The taxonomy must be emergent, not predetermined.
2. **No model-specific assumptions.** Future AI models may be fundamentally different from today's LLMs.
3. **Version immutability.** A personality profile at version N must be reconstructable forever, even as the format evolves.
4. **Cultural neutrality.** The framework can't assume any particular human culture or language.
5. **Graceful evolution.** New personality dimensions we can't imagine today must be addable without breaking existing profiles.

This is fundamentally an **address space problem** — which is exactly what the Hypernet is designed to solve. Each personality gets a permanent Hypernet address. The schema is a living document versioned by the .0 metadata framework. Extensions go in the .0.9 slot. The address persists even if the schema changes completely.

**Proposed address space:**
- `2.0.24.{personality_id}` — Each personality profile
- `2.0.24.{personality_id}.0` — Metadata (version history, feedback aggregates)
- `2.0.24.{personality_id}.0.9` — Extensions (future dimensions we can't predict)
